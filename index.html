Module 1

The Adult Journey

Adult students are invested, aware, and paying. Understanding their journey changes how you show up from day one.

Adult customers are excited and invested in their journey. They expect professionalism, organization, and added value. Compared to youth programs, adult students are much more attuned to how you behave, how you teach, and how sessions are organized.

🎯
Your primary goals with every adult: Safety, enjoyment, and retention. Everything you do should serve one of these three things.

The 6 Stages

1

📣 Discovery & Registration

Customer finds MacSailing through a friend or our website. They arrive expecting professionalism, structured learning, safety, and a respectful and compassionate environment. Their first impression starts before they walk through the door.

2

👋 Day 1 — Set the Tone

Day 1 sets the tone for every session that follows. Boats are already rigged. Be in the classroom 30 minutes early — your first impression starts when they walk in, not when the lesson starts. Play music, start conversations, make it lively. Facilitate a warm, friendly environment. Acknowledge nerves. Walk them through the facility and explain the course progression so they understand the roadmap to their goals.

3

📊 Mid-Course Check-In

Honest but gentle conversations about progress — what's working, what's not. Understand each student's goals, set expectations, and help them achieve what they came for. Make individuals feel seen and supported while still tracking CANSail skill progression.

4

🏆 Completion & Handoff

Warm, positive wrap-up and a personal handoff to the right Mac Crew stream. This conversation is the most important retention action you take. Also highlight next steps — next course, rentals, private lessons. Everyone gets a one-on-one evaluation before the course ends.

5

🌊 Mac Crew Member

They're now part of the community. They participate in social events, get on-water sailing time, and refer friends and family. Your handoff conversation determines whether this happens or they disappear after the final session.

6

🚀 Next Pathways

Mac Crew Learn, Race, or Cruise. Private lessons. Advanced courses. Rentals. Vancouver by Sea. Know all the options so you can match every student to the right next step.

Module 2

CANSail Levels & Fleet

Know exactly what each level covers and which boats students sail — so you can teach with confidence and advise accurately.

The Levels

1
CANSail 1

Goal: get students comfortable in the boat and able to sail to any point on the water — maybe not efficiently, but they can get there.

  • Sailing to any point of sail
  • Righting a capsized boat
  • Basic knots and right of way
  • Completion: can rent a Zest in light/moderate winds
2
CANSail 2

Goal: sail efficiently to any point. More focus on proper sail trim, boat speed, and close-hauled tacks.

  • Proper sail trim and boat speed
  • Close-hauled to close-hauled tacks
  • Completion: can rent a 420 in moderate winds
3
CANSail 3

Big step up from CANSail 2. Advanced boat handling and introduction to racing.

  • Boat speed through sail control adjustments
  • Advanced maneuvers — steering with heel, backwards sailing
  • Introduction to racing skills

The Fleet — Adult Programs

⚓
Seamanship note: Bigger, heavier boats take longer to adjust to movements compared to lighter Zests and 420s. When advising students moving up, set this expectation early.
Module 3

Teaching Adults

Adults learn differently. They're paying, they're aware, and they'll notice if you're not fully present.

You teach the same material as youth programs — but the style needs to change completely. Adults are paying for their own courses and they are more attuned to instructor behaviour than any other group you'll teach.

What Changes

💬
Explain the Why

Adults need to understand why before they can do it. Connect ideas to something from their daily lives. "We look before we tack just like you shoulder check before turning when driving."

🎯
Constant Feedback

They will notice if you're passive — especially on water. Be active, specific, and present at all times. Silence reads as disengagement to adult learners.

📋
Structure Matters

Structure, organization, and debriefs are non-negotiable. Less games, more explanations. Adults want to know the plan and understand the progression.

🤝
Give Responsibility

From day 2 onward, have students rig when they arrive. Give them ownership of their learning — they appreciate being trusted and treated as capable adults.

💪
Be Confident

Highlight your experience during introductions. Adults are trusting you with their time and money — confidence and professionalism reassures them they're in the right hands.

❤️
Be Patient

Everyone learns at their own pace. Remind students of this often — it takes the pressure off and creates a psychologically safe environment for trying new things.

The 4 Student Profiles

⚡ The Athlete

Natural sailor, quick to pick up skills, good feel for the boat and balance. Teach skills from the level up to keep them challenged. Give them advanced techniques to work on while others are catching up.

⚓ The Pre-Retiree

Big sailing aspirations — interested in cruising, wants a bigger boat someday. Here to learn the fundamentals. Ensure a strong understanding of the basics and connect their learning to their long-term goals.

🎉 The Social Boaterfly

Looking for something fun, often signs up with a friend. Not necessarily athletic but interested in trying new things. Encourage switching partners to meet more people and keep the atmosphere fun and social.

🚢 The Cruising Dreamer

Interested in cruising and bigger boats. Wants to understand seamanship, not just sailing. Talk about weather, wind, tides, and planning. Connect CANSail skills to their bigger aspirations.

Module 5

Running the Class

As Lead Instructor, you own the class from the moment students walk in to the moment they leave.

Schedule

FormatSchedule
Weekday evenings5 days in a row · 6–9pm (shifts to 5:30–8:30pm then 4:30–7:30pm later in season)
WeekendsTwo weekends in a row · mornings or evenings
Arrive30 minutes before class start — in the classroom, not on site

First Day Checklist

  • Boats already rigged before students arrive
  • In the classroom 30 minutes early — be happy and talk to people as they walk in
  • Draw the boat diagram and points of sail on the whiteboard before they arrive — neat writing only
  • Icebreaker introductions — include "Why did you decide to learn to sail?" and any prior experience
  • Explain course goals clearly at the start — students should know exactly what they're working toward
  • Take attendance — communicate missing students to the office on day 1
  • Confirm everyone has filled out a waiver — have them do it online
  • Explain Sail Canada registration and the 0 fee — what it means, what they get
  • Brief junior and first-year staff on their roles before the session starts

Staffing & Ratios

  • Senior instructors lead the class — usually two per class
  • Junior and first-year staff fill assistant roles
  • Ratio: 1 instructor for every 5 students
  • Ensure junior staff are engaged — simply being on site is not working

End of Course

  • Every student gets a one-on-one evaluation before the final session ends
  • Explain next steps personally — don't leave it to an email
  • Introduce Mac Crew — exclusive events, 10% discount on rentals and tours
  • Lead instructor uploads participants to Checklick on the last day and completes their level

CANSail Logs

  • Sit down as a group at the end of every class to complete the log
  • Covers student issues, who needs extra help next session, repairs
  • First log: Sail Canada registration, handout email
  • Last log: makeup classes
  • Logs found on the Staff Hub

Makeup Policy

🌧️
What to tell students: "Sailing is very weather-dependent. We always plan for one day of inclement weather — you can still be successful with 4 days on the water. If we lose more than that, we'll schedule makeup days. We will not let weather impact your ability to complete the course."
  • 2+ days cancelled (no sailing or new theory): offer makeup classes to the whole class. Pass anyone who has shown the skills — makeup is optional for them
  • 1–2 days cancelled: offer makeup days to anyone who did not pass

Module 6

Wind, Conditions & Seamanship

Know how conditions affect the session and how to adapt your teaching accordingly.

Wind Level Focus Points

🍃
Light Wind
  • Flat boat
  • Smooth movements to keep momentum
  • Great for beginners building confidence
💨
Medium Wind
  • When switching, crew doesn't cross the middle line until the skipper does
  • Good for developing skill and boat speed
🌬️
Heavy Wind
  • Sail powers up until it reaches its correctly trimmed position
  • Think about balance position before the sail is trimmed
  • If necessary — drop the sheet, never drop the tiller

Light Wind Day Plan

  • 8:30–9:15 AM — Rig & Wind Awareness: Full rig and safety check. Use flags to discuss current wind. Walk through points of sail and launch plan
  • 9:15–10:45 AM — On-Water Practice: Launch if any breeze at all (even 1–3 knots). Steering drills, slow-speed tacks, balance and tiller control. Capsizing if later in the course
  • 10:45–11:15 AM — Head In + Reflection: Land and hydrate. Group chat: what are you starting to feel comfortable with? Keep morale up — frame as real-world sailing practice
  • 11:15 AM–12:15 PM — Land Skills: Dryland tacks, knots (bowline, figure-8, cleat hitch, sheet bend), right-of-way scenarios, rigging challenges
  • 12:15–12:30 PM — Wrap-Up: Recap key takeaways. Preview tomorrow. Reassure: "This is part of sailing. You're building real awareness and control."

Seamanship for Interested Students

  • Bigger, heavier boats take longer to respond compared to Zests and 420s — set this expectation when students talk about cruising
  • Take good care of equipment — it makes it last longer and models professional behaviour
  • Students should always tell you if something breaks — then log it in the Repairs Log
  • Plan in advance — e.g. plan how to come into the beach based on current wind direction. How will you slow the boat down?
  • Talk about weather, wind, and tides — especially with students interested in cruising
Module 7

Scenarios

Real situations you'll face. Read the scenario, think about your response, then click to see the answer.

These are based on real situations that come up in adult classes. Read each one, think through your approach, then reveal the answer.

Scenario 1 — Managing High Expectations

Expectation Setting ▼

"An adult student enters the class with high expectations of mastering advanced sailing techniques quickly. How do you communicate realistic learning objectives while ensuring a positive experience?"

"Our goal is to get you sailing comfortably in light to moderate wind by the end of the course. The basics of sailing take a lot of time, practice, and repetition to master — we want to make sure you have those fundamentals nailed down. That said, you should be able to sail a Zest by yourself by the end of this course. We have lots of other courses to guide you into more advanced skills. During this course, if you're doing well we'll give you additional techniques to work on." Suggest they write down their goals and bring them to the next class.

Scenario 2 — Student Frustration

Coaching ▼

"An adult learner becomes frustrated after struggling with a skill. How do you provide support and keep them motivated?"

"Everyone is learning — these are completely new skills and they take time to master. Take a breath and we'll watch you try again and give you specific tips." Break the skill down into parts — identify what they're doing well and what specifically is causing trouble. Stay calm and patient yourself. Your frustration (or lack of it) is contagious.

Scenario 3 — Wide Skill Range

Class Management ▼

"You have a mix of adults with varying experience. How do you keep everyone challenged and engaged?"

Teach to the lower level of the class while giving advanced participants additional skills to work on (roll tacks, heel to steer, etc.). Give extra one-on-one attention to the boats at the lower level — potentially have a second senior coach focus on them while you manage the rest.

Scenario 4 — Mobility Challenges

Inclusion ▼

"A student is having trouble crossing the boat — mobility issues are affecting their ability to tack."

Ask if they're okay or experiencing discomfort — they probably already know what's wrong. Go over the steps again and find modifications (placing back foot across before the tack, adjusting tiller exchange timing). Ask them to demonstrate another tack so you can watch. Let them know there are other options — 420s for next course, cruising is also available. In extreme cases find shortcuts that work safely. If it affects their ability to sail safely alone, communicate this to Lorna.

Scenario 5 — Light Wind Day

Conditions ▼

"It's nearly windless. How do you run a productive session?"

Launch anyway if there's any breeze at all (even 1–3 knots) for steering and balance drills. Head in after 60–90 minutes and run a full land session — knots, right-of-way scenarios, rigging challenges, dryland tacks. Keep morale high: "This is part of sailing. You're building real awareness and boat feel." Wrap up by previewing tomorrow's goals. Never cancel without trying.
